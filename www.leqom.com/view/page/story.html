The Secret Behind Athletes’ Strength ?

Although the Ryukyu yam was once called the “phantom yam” due to its rarity, it suddenly drew attention in 2008. At that time, a top Jamaican sprinter who dominated the track world with overwhelming strength came into the spotlight. Global media reported that the secret behind his power might lie in the Jamaican yams he regularly ate. It was then revealed that the Ryukyu yam is a close relative of the Jamaican yam, which quickly boosted its fame. Inspired by this unique story, we began full-scale research into the sports nutrition potential of the Ryukyu yam.

Physical Challenges Faced by Athletes and People in Middle Age and Beyond

Changes in Strength and Physical Performance with Age

After middle age, it has been reported that not only muscle strength but also physical functions such as agility, endurance, and jumping ability gradually decline. This may be one of the reasons people start feeling like, “My driving distance has dropped lately,” “I can't keep up in the latter half of my workout,” or “It's getting harder to lift the barbell.”

Hormonal Changes with Age

Hormones play a role in body composition. Sex hormones serve as signals that promote protein synthesis in muscles, but it has been reported that their secretion gradually declines after middle age.

Even Young Athletes Can Have Low Hormone Levels

Even young athletes have been reported to experience a decline in sex hormones due to intense daily training. This is considered one of the key physical challenges for athletes, as it may lead to reduced performance.

LeQ’om Supports Training

In addition to training and protein intake, athletes and people in middle age can benefit from an extra boost through hormone-focused approaches. This is where the Ryukyu Yam supplement “LeQ’om” comes in to support them.

“Okinawa: A Treasure Trove of Functional Ingredients” – Carefully Cultivated Blessings of Okinawa

We visited Mr. Takara and Mr. Matsuda, Kuuga Imo farmers in Yomitan Village, about 30 km north of Naha, Okinawa.
In addition to being known for its pottery called “yachimun,” Yomitan is also known as a hub for yam cultivation. Its western side opens to scenic beaches, making it a bustling tourist area. Born and raised in Yomitan, the two men wanted to leave behind a locally distinctive crop after their retirement. They turned their attention to Kuuga Imo — a yam known for its medicinal properties and historically grown in limited regions. The moment they discovered it, they said, “This is it!” (Left: Kazuo Takara / Right: Sakae Matsuda)

The two are childhood classmates. After acquiring seed yams of Kuuga Imo, they began cultivating them — strictly without using pesticides. Kuuga Imo produces low yields, requires specific soil conditions, and has many egg-sized tubers that make harvesting extremely labor-intensive. Each one must be handled carefully, one by one.
Mr. Takara: “Every year is full of trial and error. It feels like we take three steps forward and four steps back (laughs). Still, we’ve decided to keep going so that young people can proudly cultivate this health-promoting food in the future.”

Mr. Matsuda: “When we said two old men were trying to build an industry from scratch, people laughed. But somehow, we’ve made it this far by sharing ideas with everyone. We’re taking our time, avoiding pesticides, and being as meticulous as possible.”
Their challenge continues — to deliver this heirloom food, nurtured by Okinawa’s blessings, from Okinawa to the rest of Japan and pass it on to future generations. (Photo: Kuuga Imo Field, Yomitan Village)

Functional Research Story

The success of Jamaican track athletes in the Olympics drew attention to their diet, and we began to wonder if their staple food — yams — held a secret. When we looked into whether a similar variety could be sourced domestically, we discovered a related species in Okinawa and quickly had it sent to us. That was our first encounter with Kuuga Imo. Upon analysis, we found that compared to domestic nagaimo and yamaimo, Kuuga Imo contained overwhelmingly higher levels of nutrients. We immediately felt this could be something interesting.
Our research progressed from cellular studies to animal studies, and now we’ve accumulated evidence from athletes and people in their 40s to 60s. Kuuga Imo from Okinawa is showing great promise as a food that supports sports performance and overall wellness.

Professor, College of Sport and Health Science, Ritsumeikan University
MD, Dr. Motoyuki Iemitsu

In a clinical trial (RCT) involving athletes, participants took Ryukyu Yam powder for 8 weeks along with training. The results showed that combining the supplement with training was more effective than training alone. Furthermore, a similar 12-week exercise-based RCT involving men and women in their 40s to 60s showed the same outcome. (Source: Horii, N (2020). J Clin Biochem Nutr, Nov; 67(3):338–343.)
